CSR

Corporate social responsibility (CSR) is a business approach that contributes to sustainable development by delivering economic, social and environmental benefits for all stakeholders.

About CSR

CSR is a very broad concept that addresses many and various topics such as human rights, corporate governance, health and safety, environmental effects, working conditions and contribution to economic development. CSR focus to drive change towards sustainability. 

Corporate Social Responsibility is the continuing commitment by business to behave ethically and contribute to economic development while improving the quality of life of the workforce and their families as well as of the local community and society at large.

How to apply CSR funds? To get an Empanelment with national CSR hub at the Tata Institute of Social Sciences. Through the companies we can provide funding under Corporate Social Responsibility. CSR Policy of Govt Of India

Environmental sustainability

Recycling, waste management, water management, renewable energy, reusable materials, 'greener' supply chains, reducing paper use and adopting Leadership in Energy and Environmental Design (LEED) building standards.LEED includes a set of rating systems for the design, construction, operation, and maintenance of green buildings, homes, and neighborhoods

Ethical marketing

Companies that ethically market to consumers are placing a higher value on their customers and respecting them as people who are ends in themselves. They do not try to manipulate or falsely advertise to potential consumers. This is important for companies that want to be viewed as ethical. It refers to the application of marketing ethics into the marketing process.

Triple bottom line

"People, planet and profit", also known as the triple bottom line, form one way to evaluate CSR. "People" refers to fair labour practices, the community and region where the business operates. "Planet" refers to sustainable environmental practices. Profit is the economic value created by the organization after deducting the cost of all inputs, including the cost of the capital (unlike accounting definitions of profit)
